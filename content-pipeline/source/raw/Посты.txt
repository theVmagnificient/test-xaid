How you get trapped by AI vendors’ metrics

Here are the most common pitfalls:

I recently came across an excellent paper (ESR Essentials: common performance metrics in AI - practice recommendations by the European Society of Medical Imaging Informatics). The authors highlight a simple truth: relying on a single metric is always a bad idea.

So I put together a short map of how these traps are interconnected - and why hospital and telerad executives should never buy AI based on one shiny number.

1. Sensitivity vs. PPV (Precision)

- High sensitivity looks great on paper → but if PPV is low, most “positives” are false alarms.
- Trap: Radiologists waste time chasing ghosts.

2. Specificity vs. NPV

- High specificity means few false alarms → but if NPV is weak, negative cases can’t be trusted.
- Trap: You miss patients you thought were “safe.”


3. Accuracy vs. F1-score

- Accuracy is inflated in imbalanced datasets → F1 reveals the ugly truth in rare diseases.
- Trap: “95% accuracy” AI collapses in a 3% prevalence setting.


4. AUROC vs. AUPRC

- AUROC looks impressive even in low-prevalence → AUPRC shows how bad it really is.
- Trap: Execs approve a tool that works only in theory, not in screening.


5. Dice Score (DSC) vs. Boundary Metrics (NSD/Hausdorff)

- DSC shows overlap → but ignores tiny structures or edges.
- Trap: AI “segments” the liver but misses the 3 mm metastasis.


6. Per-class metrics vs. Summary metrics

- Averages (macro/micro F1, mAP) smooth over per-class failures.
- Trap: The AI looks balanced overall but fails in exactly the subgroup you care about (e.g., small nodules).


So, remember to clearly understand what are your goals for AI in your workflow and rely on right metrics. Anything that I missed in my list?

Who should actually pay in the end?

After one of Jan Beger’s posts, I started thinking whether it’s even possible to globally make patients pay for AI - here’s where I landed.

Would it be fair to shift the price of AI onto the patient?

Maybe yes - for example, in the case of the public sector and opportunistic findings.

Take the public sector: given their fixed annual budget and KPIs, they have no incentive to spend extra money on detecting, say, opportunistic findings.

But the benefit for the patient is obvious. Does this mean the only option here is to make the patient pay? After all, they should have the right to access such a service.

The problem is that in some healthcare systems, patients are simply not used to paying for add-ons - like in certain European countries. How should tariffs be introduced in such systems without creating the impression that the state or public healthcare is neglecting them or trying to squeeze money out of them? That could even hurt political ratings.

Or should it instead be reimbursed by the government / insurance companies?

That raises the next question: is it really worth it for them, and would they scale it up at all?

Because it’s obvious that insurance companies would clearly benefit from paying for osteoporosis detection - but it’s much less obvious whether they’d want to cover coronary calcium scoring.

At the same time, it’s clear that practically all patients understand the value of AI and view it positively - but how might that perception change if they were forced to pay extra for it?

That’s the paradox.

There are multiple layers of risk in today’s teleradiology market.

After dozens of conversations with teleradiology companies, one thing became clear:
Teleradiology isn’t just outsourcing reading. It’s outsourcing risk.

Think about it: a tired radiologist at 3 AM can miss a critical finding—whether they sit inside the hospital or on the other side of the world.
The difference? The lawsuit goes to different companies.

And then there’s shadow reporting—cases quietly forwarded to cheaper countries for reading. That adds another invisible layer of risk. A layer that teleradiology companies carry, but one that can’t be insured or defended - because it’s simply illegal.

This is exactly where AI might find its natural role:
not as a competitor to radiologists in teleradiology,
but as a competitor to radiologists in shadow reporting companies.

If AI can reach the level of “shadow prereads,” it could erase that uninsured layer of risk and finally fit into the economics of teleradiology.

It’s like AI’s first competitors right now are technicians who are reading studies by themselves, not radiologists.

The 6 Deadly Sins of clinical AI Papers

We’re flooded with “AI in healthcare” papers.

But let’s be honest: most are unusable in real-world medicine.

The Lancet just published a commentary exposing the scale of the problem - and the new CHART framework is the first serious attempt to fix it.

Here are the hard truths from the systematic review of 137 chatbot health advice studies:

❌ 26% didn’t even specify which AI model was used.

❌ Only 0.7% gave enough detail to identify model version/customization.

❌ 85% had no systematic prompting method.

❌ 87% had no structured evaluation.

❌ In 65% accuracy was judged by investigator opinion rather than evidence-based guidelines.

❌ Only 32% explicitly considered patient safety.


That’s why CHART (Chatbot Assessment Reporting Tool) was created:

✅ A 12-item, 39-subitem checklist covering everything from model ID to prompting to safety.

✅ Developed through Delphi consensus with 531 stakeholders & tested for clarity.

✅ A living guideline that will evolve with multimodal models and clinical trial designs.

The choice is simple - keep publishing shiny “AI beats doctors” papers with zero reproducibility or start sticking to frameworks to really make in helpful.
