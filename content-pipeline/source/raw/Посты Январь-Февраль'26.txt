The radiologist understands where to look faster and rechecks the same areas less often.

- Free-text: 11.4 ± 4.7 s
- SR: 4.8 ± 2.6 s
- AI-SR: 3.6 ± 0.8 s
- P < 0.001

Radiologists spend ~3× less time reading text with SR and AI-SR.

SR shifts novice readers’ attention by +6.4 percentage points from text to image.

At the same time, for non-novice (experienced) readers, there was no significant change -experienced radiologists remain image-focused regardless of the reporting mode.

What do ice hockey and medicine have in common?

There’s a concept called the Peltzman Effect. To explain it clearly, let’s start with an example.

Hockey is a very injury-prone sport. To reduce injuries, the NHL introduced visors on helmets as an additional protective measure.

But paradoxically, it turned out that some players became more aggressive, increasing the risk of harm to themselves and others. In other words, adding protection actually increased the risk of injury.

The Peltzman Effect (also known as the offsetting effect) is named after economist Sam Peltzman. He proposed that humans adjust their behavior based on changes in their perceived risk. The key word here is PERCEIVED - as opposed to ACTUAL risk.

In medicine, there’s a striking example of the same phenomenon.

Researchers evaluated the impact of public reporting of quality outcomes for percutaneous coronary interventions (PCI) among Medicare beneficiaries. They compared data from 2002 to 2010 across different U.S. states. Some states publicly reported outcomes, others did not.

And here’s what they found:

In the reporting states, patients with myocardial infarction were less likely to receive PCI than in non-reporting states.

Why?

Because when data is public, physicians start worrying about their scores and outcomes - and therefore take less clinical risk. As a result, a measure that was supposed to improve quality and safety paradoxically increased risk for patients.

It’s ironic: an action taken to improve quality and reduce risk may actually increase that risk.

This is something I’ve written about multiple times. Every action we take carries risk - even when it feels completely safe. We just often fail to recognize it.

So every time you try to “improve” a system, do you stop and think that it might not make things better at all - and could easily make them worse?

If you’ve seen examples like this, share them.

Radiology is NOT Jevons’ Paradox. At least not yet.

There’s a popular argument floating around:

“AI makes radiologists faster → costs drop → imaging demand explodes → radiologists end up worse off.”

Sounds like classic Jevons’ Paradox.


I recently came across an article by Ben White where he explains why radiology doesn’t actually fit that model


1. Demand for imaging is not elastic in the economic sense.

CTs and MRIs are not consumed because they’re cheap. They’re ordered because a clinician thinks they’re necessary. Lower marginal reading cost doesn’t suddenly make doctors order scans for fun.



2. radiology is supply-constrained, not demand-constrained.

The bottleneck is radiologists, scanners, technologists, slots in the schedule. Even if AI makes reporting faster, you don’t magically get more scanners, more staff, or more hours in a day.



3. time savings don’t automatically convert into volume.

A radiologist reading 20% faster doesn’t mean 20% more scans get done. It often means:

- slightly shorter turnaround time

- slightly less backlog

- slightly less burnout


None of these trigger runaway demand.


4. reimbursement is the real brake.

In many systems, imaging volumes are capped or tightly controlled by payers. If AI makes reads cheaper, the savings usually go to the system - not into ordering more scans.

So what does AI actually do in radiology today?

It shifts where the pain is:

- from impossible turnaround times

- to capacity redistribution

- to keeping the system from breaking under growth it already has

That’s damage control, not Jevons’ Paradox


The paradox might appear later - if:

- AI removes radiologists from the loop entirely

- scanners become the only remaining bottleneck

- and reimbursement models fundamentally change


Maybe AI is not creating demand but rather absorbing it now?

Prompting just beat fine-tuning

You no longer need to suffer with data labelling and complex training.
Here’s an example of a task that consumes up to 6% of radiologists’ time.


In a study based on 1,448 real CT requisitions, expert radiologists defined a “reference” protocol for each case. The authors then compared how protocols were selected by:

1. Humans (residents, fellows, attendings)

2. GPT-4o with a very detailed prompt

3. GPT-4o after fine-tuning - requiring additional training and labeled data


The interesting part: GPT with a detailed prompt selected the optimal protocol in 96.2% of cases, while humans did so in 88.3%.

The number of errors was similarly low for both humans and GPT (around 1–2%).

But here’s the key finding: fine-tuning GPT did not improve performance at all compared to GPT using context-rich prompting only.

Overall, the paper is a great example of a task that genuinely drains time and energy, yet can be almost fully automated without training, data labeling, or classical ML pipelines.
