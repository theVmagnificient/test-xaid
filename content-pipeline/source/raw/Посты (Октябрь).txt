31.10.25
Higher malpractice payouts - fewer scans???


A new AJR study analyzing 630,000 emergency department headache cases across all US states found something counterintuitive:

where malpractice payouts were higher, doctors ordered fewer scans.

Main findings of the study:

- In states with damage caps, the likelihood of ordering any imaging was 21% lower.

- In states with several liability (where each defendant is responsible only for their share of damages), it was even lower -> −32%.


And the most counterintuitive part - higher average malpractice payouts were also associated with lower CT and overall imaging rates. For every $10,000 increase in average malpractice payment, imaging odds dropped 1%.


At first glance, it should be the other way around.


But possibly this is because:

1. High payouts ≠ high lawsuit risk.

Such states may have stricter legal frameworks and filters, so the actual risk of being sued is lower, and physicians are better protected.

2. High payouts indicate a more mature insurance system.

They might simply reflect a better-functioning legal and compensation environment, not a more aggressive one.

3. Medicaid context.

In the Medicaid population, imaging may already be more tightly regulated due to lower margins and reimbursement levels.

4. Correlation ≠ causation.

The authors note this themselves - we can’t say that higher payouts caused fewer CTs; it’s more likely that both coexist within more developed, legally and organizationally mature healthcare systems where doctors don’t practice “scan everything” medicine.



More numbers:

- The strongest non-legal predictor: if the ordering provider was not an emergency physician, the likelihood of imaging was 8.5× higher.

- In smaller hospitals (<100 beds), imaging was 35% less likely.

So it always comes down to context - the system in which physicians make decisions.

Perhaps with a better-tuned system, we could safely reduce unnecessary CTs.

Or perhaps not - because at the end of the day, the patient’s life comes first, and we must see how such changes would actually affect outcomes.

AI that monitors AI


Yesterday at the RSNA Spotlight Course on Radiology AI, the topic of continuous monitoring of AI in production came up again.


For comprehensive models, the main approach that currently looks viable is LLM-based monitoring - comparing radiologists’ reports with AI-generated ones.

It’s not perfect: there are multiple points in the comparison chain where AI itself can fail.

But still, it’s the most promising path for now - especially if you focus on the most critical pathologies first, without overcomplicating things with weights or multi-class balancing.


Then I came across a new paper from Stanford that takes a completely different route.


Researchers proposed training a set of sub-models for the same task as the commercial AI model (in this case - intracranial hemorrhage detection).

Then they compare outputs in real time — how much the sub-models agree with the main model.


The results?

-> 92% F1 score for real-time confidence estimation.
-> Cases with low agreement = most likely AI errors.

Seems great.

But the real question is how to implement this at scale in real clinical practice.

Because for every pathology, you’d need to train new models.

And what if your system covers 20+ findings?

What if there’s no open dataset?

And how do you handle data drift?

But the approach itself is interesting - if those challenges can be solved.

And maybe, it could even be combined with the LLM-based monitoring approach - bringing together statistical confidence and semantic understanding into one loop.

With a single pathology, it’s simple: yes/no.

But with many findings, should different errors carry different weights?

Should false positives and false negatives be penalized equally? Even within those, shouldn’t severity matter?

And context matters too - on-call vs. routine settings may demand completely different weighting of the same errors.

Curious to hear how others imagine the ideal AI monitoring framework for multi-pathology systems.

No, that’s not a joke -

that’s the average radiologist sensitivity for detecting ischemic stroke on non-contrast CT.
But AI isn’t doing much better either - 59.8% on external validation.

This comes from a recent meta-analysis on ischemic stroke detection in NCCT.

Here’s what they found:

AI performance:

- Internal validation → 91.2% sensitivity, 96.0% specificity
- External validation → sensitivity collapses to 59.8%

Human radiologists:

- Sensitivity: 44.1% (internal) / 46.1% (external)

Clinicians + AI:
- Sensitivity jumps to 83.7%, specificity 86.7%

→ Nearly double either human-only or AI-only detection.

And how do we actually study human-AI interaction - because that kind of metric jump deserves some real explanation.
